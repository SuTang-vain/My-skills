# Source Priority

Use this file when several possible citations exist and you need to choose the best link.

## Priority Order

Rank candidate sources in this order unless the task gives a stronger reason to do otherwise:

1. Original primary source
2. Official first-party summary or distribution page
3. High-quality secondary reporting that directly cites the primary source
4. Tertiary summaries or commentary

## By Claim Type

### Academic papers

Prefer:

1. Publisher or journal landing page
2. DOI resolver page
3. arXiv or other recognized preprint server
4. Author, lab, or university page hosting the paper or supplement

Avoid relying only on blog posts that paraphrase the paper unless the user explicitly wants commentary.

### Statistics and datasets

Prefer:

1. Official dataset or database
2. Official report that publishes the figure
3. Regulator, standards body, or government release
4. Reputable analysis that reproduces the figure and links upstream

Check that:

- the date matches the claim
- the unit matches the claim
- the geography or population matches the claim

### Companies, products, and business facts

Prefer:

1. Company filing, investor report, official press release, or product documentation
2. Official company blog or newsroom
3. Reputable reporting that quotes the primary source

Use caution with marketing pages if the claim is promotional rather than factual.

### Events and timelines

Prefer:

1. Official announcement, court filing, regulator notice, standards update, or organization statement
2. Contemporaneous reporting from a reputable outlet
3. Retrospective explainers that clearly cite the original event record

Check the exact date. If the user says "today" or "recently", convert that into explicit dates when answering.

## Confidence Labels

- `High`: The source directly states the claim or is the original record.
- `Medium`: The source strongly supports the claim but through summary, aggregation, or close paraphrase.
- `Low`: The source is indirect, incomplete, old, or only partially aligned with the claim.

## Red Flags

Treat a source as weak and say so when:

- it repeats a number without showing where it came from
- it summarizes a paper without linking the paper
- the date, geography, or unit does not match
- the article cites a derivative chart but not the underlying data
- the claim is causal but the source only shows correlation

## Failure Mode

If you cannot confirm a reliable source:

- do not invent a citation
- mark the claim as unverified
- explain what kind of source would be needed to verify it
